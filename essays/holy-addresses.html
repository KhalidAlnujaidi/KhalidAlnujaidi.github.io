01 / the construct  ·  one word, three jobs

“Address” is doing three jobs at once. The overlap is the whole idea.

A tradition's answer to the first sense quietly settles the other two. Where God can be found determines how God is hailed — and how the body must stand to hail it. Three questions that turn out to be one.

sense one · location

A place to be found

like a postal address

Does God occupy a findable spot — a summit, a heaven, a cult-statue, a sanctum?

Does God have one?
sense two · direction

To hail, to direct speech

to address an assembly

How is God turned toward and spoken to — by climbing to a presence, or by facing a sign?

How is God hailed?
sense three · bearing

The body before the act

addressing the ball, in golf or archery

How does the worshipper stand — raised and upward, or oriented and lowered?

How must the body bear?

The usual reading of sacred architecture opposes vertical and horizontal forms and stops there — as if the axis were the variable. It isn't. The axis is a consequence. The real variable is the first sense: locatability. A findable God yields a vertical address — climb to where He is. An unfindable one yields a horizontal-and-prostrate address — orient toward a sign, and lower yourself, because there is nowhere to climb to.

02 / the address of the located god

It was never “up vs. grounded.” It’s whether there’s a summit to climb to at all.

The ziggurat is an artificial mountain — zaqāru, “to build high” — with the god's chamber at the summit. The Pyramid Texts send pharaoh up the sun's rays to join Ra. When the sacred has an address, architecture's job is mediation: build the staircase, put the priest-king on it. Height is the theology.

Tawhīd makes God omnipresent. “Wherever you turn, there is the Face of God” (2:115); “We are closer to him than his jugular vein” (50:16). Because God is everywhere — and nearer to you than you are to yourself — there is no summit to reach, and climbing becomes a category error. The Qur'an says so with open satire, putting the tower in the mouth of Pharaoh, who orders Hāmān to build a ṣarḥ "that I may look upon the God of Moses" (40:36–37). The problem shifts from ascent to orientation.

03 / the address of the non-located god  ·  the axis flips here

Verticality wasn't deleted. It was demoted from the interface.

Islam keeps the vertical in its cosmology — the Miʿrāj is an ascent, the Kaʿba sits beneath al-Bayt al-Maʿmūr, which the angels circle. But it removes the vertical from the ritual interface. And then it inverts the embodied prior outright: the moment of greatest nearness is sajda, the lowest posture — "the servant is nearest to his Lord while prostrating" (Muslim).

That inversion is why it bites. Psychology has measured a hard "God = up" reflex in the body (the cognitive default, below). Sajda spends effort against that reflex — and costly rituals tend to be the binding ones.

04 / who has been here — four literatures that rarely cite each other

Every piece exists. Nobody has assembled them.

Tap a field. The striking part isn't any single claim — it's that these four bodies of work sit in separate rooms. The art historians rarely read the cognitive scientists; the anthropologists rarely read the Sufis. The centre of the diagram is empty on purpose.

+
Islamic art history

The Kaʿba as orientation, not altar

O'Meara · Burckhardt · Nasr · Shariati

O'Meara's The Kaʿba Orientations (2020) — the first English monograph on it — treats it not as a building to interpret but through what it does in space: it fixes the qibla, and it is a house built around an emptiness. He names two vectors — horizontal toward the Kaʿba, and through it vertical toward God. The thesis, in academic dress.

Burckhardt: its emptiness reveals presence as limitlessness — no sacramental centre like a Christian altar. Nasr ties circumambulation to squaring the circle. Shariati: an empty cube facing all directions and therefore none.

+
Comparative religion · a complication

Mecca is also an axis mundi

Eliade · Clifford · the cosmic-mountain school

This school dissolves the binary rather than backing it. The ziggurat is an artificial holy mountain, the priest ascends the summit to meet the god — but in the same framework Mecca is a cosmic centre too, an axis mundi in Sufi cosmology. Where the thesis sees rupture, Eliade sees continuity.

Worth knowing this is contested: J.Z. Smith argued the "universal axis mundi" was over-read into the evidence. Either way, it's the strongest objection to the clean contrast — and the reason the next section exists.

+
Embodied cognition

“God = up” is measurable, not metaphor

Meier, Hauser, Robinson et al. · JPSP 2007

Six experiments: implicit God→up / Devil→down associations; people encode God-concepts faster in high screen positions; memory recalls divine images higher than they were. Replications (2021–23) held for the core representation effect.

So verticality isn't only a cultural choice — it's a cognitive default sitting in the perceptual system. That's what makes sajda's downward inversion a costly move.

+
Anthropology · statistics

The sky-god is coded into the dataset

Murdock's Ethnographic Atlas · “High Gods” · Botero 2014

Cross-cultural databases literally encode the variable as “High Gods” — a being that created or governs all reality. Studies across 583 societies correlate such belief with ecology and social complexity (harsher environments, more cooperation pressure → big watching gods).

The "up" is baked into the vocabulary of comparative religion before any analysis begins. Verticality is the field's default unit.

05 / the real variable

The real variable isn't monotheism. It's mediation.

Test the naive claim and it breaks immediately: Gothic Christianity is rigorously monotheist and maximally vertical — pointed arches and spires as literal "spiritual ascension." If monotheism caused grounding, Chartres couldn't exist.

So swap the variable. The predictor is mediation theology. Incarnation means God entered vertical space — the ladder stays coherent, you can still climb toward a God who came down. Tawhīd without a mediator collapses the ladder, leaving pure orientation. That's a sharper claim than the original — and one you can actually hold up against cases, which the next section does.

A monotheism that puts God in a body keeps its spires. A monotheism that puts God everywhere keeps only its compass.

verticality tracks mediation — not monotheism. Gothic & Kaʿba are both monotheist; they split on whether God sits at a place you can climb to.
06 / the part you can actually watch

Take one tradition. Remove its address. Watch the geometry move.

The civilizational contrast — pyramid vs. cube — invites cherry-picking. The stronger move holds a single tradition fixed and changes only how locatable its God is, then watches the form respond. Same God, datable rupture, predictable shift: lose the location → gain orientation.

case · A

Judaism after the Temple

70 CE

Second-Temple worship was aniconic but high-mediation: the divine Presence — the Shekhinah — dwelt in a located sanctum, the Holy of Holies, behind the veil, above the Ark. Then Rome destroyed the Temple, and the address was gone.

Worship reorganized around facing Jerusalem — a shift from a resident presence to bare direction, the same structure as the qibla, reached independently. Lose the location, gain orientation.

case · B

The Protestant Reformation

16th c.

Same God, same scripture — but mediation deliberately cut: real presence rejected or weakened, saints and images stripped, the mediating priesthood dissolved ("the priesthood of all believers"). The prediction is a matching de-verticalization of the worship space.

→
word · gathering
The pulpit, central

The pulpit (the Word) displaces the altar (presence) as the focal point; the auditory church replaces the ascent toward a sanctum. Mediation drops, verticality drops — with monotheism, scripture, and century all held constant.

Two ruptures, one direction. That's what turns this from a poem about distant civilizations into something with a moving part you can watch — and, just as important, something a clear counterexample could break.

07 / the cognitive substrate

Verticality is the attractor. The cube is the residual.

Gravity, the infant's upward gaze, dominance posture, the sky where light and rain come from — the "up" is the low-energy state that human sacred space keeps falling into. Read this way, the pyramid isn't interesting; it's the default. The Kaʿba is interesting as a tradition that climbed out of the attractor — kept the vertical in cosmology, but deliberately engineered the ritual to point sideways and bow down.

an embodied energy well. Most traditions settle at the bottom (vertical). Staying on the ridge takes engineering — and costs effort, which is the point.
08 / the topology  ·  same idea, drawn as graphs

Pyramid = a tree with an apex. Kaʿba = a star graph on a sphere.

The pyramid stores its monumentality in one object — mass, labour, a costly signal of a centralized state, with a survivorship bias toward stone. The Kaʿba's monumentality is distributed: across every qibla wall on the planet. One is a hierarchy with a single privileged node. The other is a global vector field — one convergence point, every node equal in rank.

tree · single apex node
power flows down from one privileged point. Rank is the structure.
star graph · every node equal
one convergence point, no rank. The monument is the whole field at once.
09 / so — is this “sacred geometry”?

Yes — but only half of that term carries weight.

There's a genuine metaphysical lineage here, and there's New-Age decoration wearing its clothes. The load-bearing idea is squaring the circle: the circle as unmanifest spirit-space, the square as the manifest world. When you draw them near-equal, "the infinite expresses itself through the finite" (Nasr's lineage). Circumambulation is a circle walked around a cube — heaven traced around earth. Mosque design says it again structurally: a dome (circle) descending onto a cube (square) through squinches — heaven coming down rather than man building up. Same inversion as sajda, in stone.

the circumambulating point orbits the cube — the rite is the construction.
load-bearing — keep
  • Squaring the circle. A real geometric-cosmological tradition (Nasr, Critchlow); circle=spirit, square=world.
  • Plato's cube = earth. The Timaeus assigns the cube to earth precisely for its stability. The Kaʿba's form is the element of ground.
  • Aniconic pattern as theology. Islamic geometric ornament dissolves the image the way dhikr dissolves fixation — form standing in for the unrepresentable.
  • Dome-on-cube. Heaven (circle) lowered onto earth (square) — buildable, legible, structurally true.
decoration — handle with care
  • “Cube energy in the 7th dimension.” The crystal-grid material that surfaces in searches. Unfalsifiable; not part of the classical tradition.
  • Numerology as proof. “72 = 12 × 6, therefore…” — pattern-matching dressed as meaning.
  • Universal axis mundi as fact. Even Eliade's version is contested (J.Z. Smith). Useful as a lens, not a law.
  • Sacred proportion everywhere. Golden-ratio-hunting finds what it wants to find. Demand the primary source first.

The honest position: an argument like this does live in sacred geometry — the serious, square-and-circle, Platonic-solid wing of it — and gains nothing by borrowing from the crystal-energy wing. The left column is where the weight sits. The right is decoration.

10 / what’s new here — and what isn’t

Sorting the fresh move from the established scholarship.

A map for the skeptic: where this reading is an original framing, where it’s connective synthesis, and where it leans on prior work that’s open to dispute.

the framing — the original move

The construct of divine address itself: treating locatability (does God have a findable place?) as the primitive, and the vertical/horizontal axis as its consequence rather than the variable — so the three senses of "address" (a place, a hailing, a bearing) collapse into one question. Posed this sharply, the pyramid-vs-cube contrast isn't the organizing question anywhere in the literature; O'Meara names "two vectors," Burckhardt names "orientation," but the coupling is the fresh move — a diffuse intuition compressed into a shape you can test.

the synthesis — built, not cited

Claims that don't come from any single source — the connective tissue rather than the footnotes:

mediation theology, not monotheism, as the real predictor the two within-tradition tests — Temple loss & the Reformation verticality as cognitive attractor → Kaʿba as residual tree-with-apex vs star-graph-on-a-sphere power-in-the-object vs power-in-the-network sajda as a costly move against a measured bias

The within-tradition cases are the strongest of these — they isolate mediation from monotheism across a datable rupture. They're syntheses rather than retrievals, which is what makes them worth writing up, and the part most open to challenge.

the prior art — borrowed, and contested

The "God = up" prior is Meier's, measured in 2007. Kaʿba-as-orientation is Burckhardt / Shariati / O'Meara. Squaring-the-circle is Nasr's lineage. And the binary itself is partly dissolved by the evidence: Eliade reads Mecca as an axis mundi too, and Gothic is monotheist and maximally vertical. The framing holds at the level of the ritual interface — but it survives the Gothic counterexample only once the mediation patch is swapped in. That patch is what turns it from evocative into something a critic can actually argue with.

11 /  a lens, not a verdict

The geometry of divine address

Offered as a thought experiment, not a proof — a way of reading sacred space that happens to have a moving part you can check, and an honest pile of objections built in.
